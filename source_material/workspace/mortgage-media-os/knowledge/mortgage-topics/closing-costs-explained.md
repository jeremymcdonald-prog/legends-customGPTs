# Closing Costs — The Honest Breakdown

This is the conversation nobody has with buyers until it's almost too late. Let me have it with you now, before you find yourself surprised at the closing table.

---

## The Reality

On a $350,000 home purchase, your total buyer closing costs will typically run $7,000-$17,500. That's 2%-5% of the purchase price, in addition to your down payment.

Most first-time buyers don't know this exists until 3 days before closing. I'm telling you now.

---

## What Is In Closing Costs (Itemized)

**Lender Fees:**
- Loan origination fee: 0.5%-1% of loan amount ($1,750-$3,500 on a $350K loan)
- Underwriting fee: $300-$750
- Application fee: $200-$500
- Credit report: $35

**Third-Party Fees:**
- Home appraisal: $500-$1,000+
- Home inspection: $300-$500 (paid before closing, not at closing)
- Title search: $300-$600
- Title insurance — lender's policy: $500-$1,500
- Title insurance — owner's policy: $500-$2,000 (optional but strongly recommended)
- Recording fees: $50-$250
- Survey: $300-$700 (sometimes required)

**Prepaid Items (Not Fees — Money Going Into Escrow or Prepaid Interest):**
- Prepaid mortgage interest (days from closing to end of month)
- Homeowner's insurance first year: $1,200-$2,500+ (Florida insurance is expensive)
- Property tax escrow: 2-6 months of taxes set aside
- HOA dues if applicable

## The Seller Concession Strategy

Here's the part most buyers don't know: in many market conditions, you can ask the seller to pay your closing costs. These are called "seller concessions."

**Maximum Seller Concessions Allowed:**
- Conventional (less than 10% down): 3%
- Conventional (10%-24% down): 6%
- Conventional (25%+ down): 9%
- FHA: 6%
- VA: 4% (plus unlimited for certain items like paying down the buyer's debt)
- USDA: 6%

**How to use this:** In a buyer-friendly negotiation, you offer $5,000-$10,000 more than asking price with the seller crediting that amount back to your closing costs. Net cost to you: the same purchase price. Net benefit: your out-of-pocket at closing drops by the concession amount.

This strategy works best when:
- The market has softened (seller needs to sell)
- The home has been on the market 30+ days
- The appraisal supports the higher purchase price

I help my clients structure this negotiation on every transaction where it makes sense.

## Lender Credits — Trading Rate for Cash

This is another tool most buyers never hear about: you can accept a slightly higher interest rate in exchange for the lender paying some or all of your closing costs.

Example: Instead of locking at 6.35% and paying $8,000 in closing costs, you lock at 6.55% and the lender provides a $5,000 credit toward closing costs. You pay less upfront, and over time the slightly higher rate costs you less than the cash you saved.

This makes sense when:
- You're tight on cash for closing
- You plan to refinance when rates drop (so the higher rate is temporary)
- Your breakeven on keeping cash vs. paying costs is less than your expected time in the loan

I run this math for every client. The right answer depends on your specific situation.

## How To Reduce Closing Costs

1. **Negotiate seller concessions** — most effective tactic available
2. **Accept lender credits** — trade rate for cash
3. **Shop title companies** — in Florida, you can often choose your own title company; shopping can save $500-$1,500
4. **Close at month end** — reduces prepaid interest days to almost zero
5. **Challenge the appraisal fee** — some lenders use in-house appraisers at lower cost

---

## What Never Changes and What Is Negotiable

**Not negotiable:**
- Recording fees (set by county government)
- Transfer taxes (set by state)
- Prepaid interest (based on closing date)
- Escrow reserves (standard calculation)

**Negotiable or shoppable:**
- Lender origination fees
- Title insurance (in Florida)
- Survey (sometimes waived with title insurance)
- Lender's underwriting fee
