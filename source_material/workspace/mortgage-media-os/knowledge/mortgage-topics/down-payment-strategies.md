# Down Payment Strategies — The Real Options

The 20% down myth has kept more people renting than any other piece of bad financial advice. Let me clear this up and give you the real playbook.

---

## The Truth About 20% Down

You do NOT need 20% down to buy a home. That number comes from one place: avoiding PMI on a conventional loan. That's it. It has nothing to do with what you're actually required to bring to the table.

The real minimums in 2026:

| Loan Type | Minimum Down | PMI/MIP |
|---|---|---|
| VA | 0% | None, ever |
| USDA | 0% | Small annual fee |
| FHA | 3.5% (580+ credit) | For life of loan (unless 10%+ down) |
| Conventional | 3% | Until 20% equity reached |
| DSCR/Investor | 20%-25% | None |

On a $350,000 home, 3.5% is $12,250. 20% is $70,000. The gap between "waiting to save 20%" and "buying now with 3.5% FHA" could be years of equity-building and appreciation you never recapture.

## Strategy 1: Government Loan Programs (VA / FHA / USDA)

**VA:** The gold standard for eligible veterans and active duty. Zero down, no PMI, competitive rates. If you qualify, this is almost always the right choice.

**FHA:** 3.5% down with a 580+ credit score. The go-to for buyers who need flexibility on credit or DTI. The trade-off is lifetime mortgage insurance, but the FHA-to-conventional refinance strategy eliminates it once you've built equity and improved your credit.

**USDA:** Zero down for eligible rural and some suburban areas. Northeast Florida has USDA-eligible areas — Nassau County and parts of St. Johns County qualify. Income limits apply. Worth checking if you're buying in those zones.

## Strategy 2: Florida Down Payment Assistance Programs

**Hometown Heroes Program:**
- Up to $35,000 in down payment and closing cost assistance
- Zero interest, deferred repayment (repaid when you sell, refinance, or move)
- For full-time Florida workers in eligible professions (including teachers, nurses, law enforcement, firefighters, and now expanded to other workers)
- Income and purchase price limits apply

**FL Assist:**
- Up to $10,000, zero interest, deferred

**FL HFA Preferred Grant:**
- 3%-5% of loan amount as a second mortgage
- Forgiven 20% per year — fully forgiven after 5 years if you stay

**How to access these:** They're layered on top of your primary mortgage. You need a participating lender (I'm one). The process is slightly more involved but the savings are real.

## Strategy 3: Gift Funds

FHA allows 100% of the down payment to come from gift funds — a parent, grandparent, spouse, or qualifying employer. The gift must be properly documented (gift letter, source of funds verification, proof of transfer).

Conventional also allows gift funds, typically for the portion exceeding the minimum borrower contribution.

---

## Strategy 4: Down Payment Savings Accelerators

If you're building toward a down payment, these are the highest-leverage tactics:

**Automate the savings.** Set up a separate savings account and auto-transfer the target amount on payday. Money you never see in checking never gets spent.

**High-yield savings accounts (HYSA):** Savings earning 4%-5% APY beat a standard savings account by $400-$500/year on a $10,000 balance. Move your down payment savings here.

**The rental savings trigger:** Calculate the difference between what you currently pay in rent and what your estimated mortgage payment would be. Save that difference every month. It builds both the habit and the account.

**Retirement account loans (plan-specific):** Some 401(k) plans allow first-time homebuyer withdrawals with reduced penalties. Check with your plan administrator — rules vary.

---

## Strategy 5: Equity from a Current Property

If you already own a home with equity, options include:
- **Cash-out refinance** on the current home to fund the down payment on the next
- **HELOC (Home Equity Line of Credit)** — draw on equity as needed
- **Bridge loan** — short-term financing between the sale of one home and close of the next
- **Sale contingency** — sell current home, use proceeds for down payment (requires careful timing)

## The Real Question: How Much Should You Put Down?

More down is not always better. Here's how I think about it:

**Put down more if:**
- Eliminating PMI at closing improves your monthly cash flow significantly
- A larger down payment drops you into a better rate tier
- You have more than enough cash reserves after the down payment

**Put down less if:**
- The cash is better deployed in a high-yield savings or investment account
- Down payment assistance programs cover the gap
- The rate difference between 5% and 20% down is minimal
- Preserving cash reserves is a priority (a 6-month emergency fund protects against everything)

I run the math both ways for every client. The right answer is specific to you.
