# Conventional Loans: The Full Picture

## What a Conventional Loan Actually Is

Here's something that trips people up. "Conventional" doesn't mean easy or standard. It just means the loan isn't backed by a government agency like FHA, VA, or USDA. Instead, it follows guidelines set by Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac, the two government-sponsored enterprises that buy most mortgages from lenders.

That matters to you because it affects the rules, the costs, and who qualifies. Conventional loans are private market loans, which means lenders can be pickier, but the tradeoffs are often worth it, especially if your credit and finances are solid.

## The Big Advantages in 2026

**PMI goes away.** This is the one that gets people excited. With conventional loans, once you hit 20% equity, private mortgage insurance (PMI) automatically cancels. With FHA, that mortgage insurance premium sticks around for the life of the loan if you put less than 10% down. On a $350,000 loan, that PMI savings alone can be $100-$200 per month once you hit 20% equity.

**No property restrictions.** Conventional loans will finance properties that FHA or VA won't touch, including condos in non-approved complexes, investment properties, second homes, and homes that need some work.

**Higher loan limits.** The 2026 conforming loan limit is $806,500 for most of the country, including most of Northeast Florida. That's the threshold for a standard conventional loan. Above that, you're into jumbo territory with different rules.

**Flexible down payment options.** Yes, you can do conventional with as little as 3% down (Fannie Mae HomeReady and Freddie Mac Home Possible programs). You don't need 20%.

## What You Need to Qualify

Let me give you the real numbers, not the teaser version.

**Credit Score:**
- 620 minimum to qualify at most lenders
- 660+ to get decent rates
- 700+ is where you start competing for the best pricing
- 740+ unlocks the best rate tiers

**Down Payment:**
- 3% minimum (with income-based programs)
- 5% standard minimum
- 10-20% eliminates PMI from day one

**Debt-to-Income Ratio (DTI):**
- 45% is the standard maximum
- Up to 50% with strong compensating factors (big reserves, high credit score)
- Front-end ratio (housing only) should ideally be under 28%

**Employment and Income:**
- Two years same field preferred
- Self-employed borrowers need two years of tax returns
- Commission income averaged over two years
- Rental income typically counted at 75% of gross rents

## The PMI Reality

If you put less than 20% down, you'll pay PMI. Here's what it actually costs in 2026 on a $350,000 home:

- 5% down: approximately $175-$200/month PMI
- 10% down: approximately $110-$130/month PMI
- 15% down: approximately $65-$85/month PMI

But here's what I tell every client: PMI isn't forever. Once you reach 20% equity, you can request cancellation. At 22% equity, the lender is required by law to remove it automatically. So you're paying for time, not forever. Compare that to FHA MIP which can follow you for the entire loan term.

## The 3% Down Programs Most People Don't Know About

Fannie Mae HomeReady and Freddie Mac Home Possible are the two big ones. They allow 3% down for qualifying borrowers, and they have income limits (generally 80% of area median income) that make them designed for middle-income buyers.

What's cool about HomeReady is that you can count income from non-borrower household members, like a parent or roommate, as a compensating factor. That helps people with unusual household situations qualify.

In Jacksonville and Northeast Florida, the 2026 area median income is around $85,000, so the 80% threshold works out to roughly $68,000 for most programs. That covers a lot of first-time buyers.

## Conventional Loans for Investment Properties

This is where conventional really shines for investors. You can finance up to 10 properties with conventional loans (with some portfolio rules). The typical requirements for investment property:

- 15-25% down (varies by property type)
- 680+ credit score
- Reserve requirement: 6 months PITI per investment property
- Rental income can offset the payment in qualifying

For a second home, you typically need 10% down and the property can't be a rental.

## Who Conventional Is Perfect For

- Buyers with 660+ credit scores who want flexibility
- People buying non-FHA-eligible properties (condos, fixer-uppers)
- Borrowers who don't want permanent mortgage insurance
- Investors buying rental properties or second homes
- Move-up buyers with equity to put 20%+ down
- High-income borrowers who exceed FHA limits

## Who Might Be Better Off Elsewhere

- Veterans: VA loan is almost always better (zero down, no PMI, competitive rates)
- Rural buyers with moderate income: USDA can beat conventional
- Buyers with credit below 620: FHA is the path
- Buyers with limited savings: Down payment assistance programs exist for FHA, not typically conventional
