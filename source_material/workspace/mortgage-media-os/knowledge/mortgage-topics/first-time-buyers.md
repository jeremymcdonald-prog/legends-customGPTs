## The Fears Are Real — Let's Address Them Head-On

Before I explain the process, let me acknowledge what most first-time buyers are actually feeling:

**"What if I don't qualify?"** This fear keeps more people in rent than anything else. The honest answer: you might not qualify today — and that's okay. My job is to tell you exactly what it would take and how long it would realistically take to get there. I've had clients go from "not ready" to "closed on their dream home" in 6 months. Knowing the path removes the fear of the unknown.

**"What if I choose the wrong house?"** The process is designed to protect you. You have an inspection contingency, an appraisal contingency, and a financing contingency. These are your escape hatches if something isn't right. You don't have to be trapped.

**"I don't have 20% down."** Good news: you don't need it. FHA loans require 3.5% down. Conventional loans go as low as 3%. VA loans go all the way to zero. And Florida has assistance programs that can cover some or all of your down payment if you qualify.

**"The process is too complicated."** It was designed by bureaucrats, so yes, it is complicated. That's why you hire a broker. My job is to make it simple for you. You focus on finding a home you love. I handle the paperwork.

## The Five Steps — Plain English Version

### Step 1: Talk to a Lender Before You Look at Houses

I know this feels backwards. But here is what happens when people skip this step: they fall in love with a house they can't afford, or they qualify for $75,000 more than they thought and settle for something they don't love.

A pre-approval letter tells you exactly what you can borrow, what your payment will be, and what the seller needs to see to take your offer seriously. Without it, no serious seller will even consider your offer.

**Pre-qualification vs. pre-approval:** Pre-qualification is a rough estimate based on what you tell me. Pre-approval is a verified commitment based on what I verify. Always get pre-approval before you make an offer.

### Step 2: Get Crystal Clear on Your Budget (Not Just the Mortgage Payment)

Your mortgage payment is one number. Your real housing cost is a different, larger number. Here is what first-time buyers consistently underestimate:

- Property taxes (in Florida, often $2,000-$6,000 per year)
- Homeowner's insurance ($1,200-$2,500+ per year in Florida)
- HOA fees (if applicable, $0-$500+ per month)
- Maintenance and repairs (budget 1%-2% of home value annually)
- Utilities (often 30%-50% higher in a house than an apartment)

If your mortgage payment is $1,800 but your total housing cost is $2,500, that's the number you need to stress-test against your income.

### Step 3: Find a Realtor, Then Find a Home

A buyer's agent costs you nothing — the seller pays the commission. A good one is invaluable. They know the market, they negotiate on your behalf, and they steer you away from homes that look great online and fall apart in person.

Once you have the agent and the pre-approval letter, start shopping. Your agent submits an offer when you find the right one.

### Step 4: Contract to Close — Here's What Happens

Once your offer is accepted:
- **Home inspection** (Days 1-7): An independent inspector checks everything. This is your chance to discover problems and either negotiate repairs, a price reduction, or walk away.
- **Appraisal** (Days 7-21): The lender orders an independent appraisal to confirm the home is worth what you're paying. If it comes in low, you can negotiate with the seller or walk away.
- **Underwriting** (Days 14-28): The lender verifies all your documents — income, assets, credit, employment. This is where people sometimes panic because the underwriter asks for more documents. That's normal. Just respond quickly and honestly.
- **Clear to close** (Days 25-35): The golden words. Your loan is approved and you're getting the keys.

### Step 5: Closing Day

You sit down, you sign about 100 pages of documents, you wire your closing costs and remaining down payment, and then someone hands you keys. That's it. You own a home.

## What Nobody Tells You at the Bank

**The rate they quote is not the rate you have to take.** Banks offer their rate. Brokers shop 40+ lenders and find the best rate available for your situation. On a $300,000 loan, a 0.5% difference in rate is roughly $90 per month — that's $32,400 over the life of the loan.

**Seller concessions are real.** In a buyer-friendly market, you can ask the seller to pay some or all of your closing costs. I've negotiated full closing cost coverage for first-time buyers. If nobody tells you to ask for it, you won't ask for it.

**Down payment assistance exists.** Florida's Hometown Heroes program offers up to $35,000 in down payment and closing cost assistance to qualifying full-time Florida workers. The FL HFA programs offer forgivable second mortgages. If nobody tells you to ask for these, you'll pay out of pocket for something you didn't have to.

**Your credit score matters a lot — but not as much as you think.** FHA goes down to 580. Conventional is workable at 620. VA has no official minimum. The people who tell you that you need 750+ to get a good mortgage are usually trying to sell you a credit repair service.

## Common First-Time Buyer Mistakes

1. **Making a major purchase before closing.** A new car, a furniture purchase on credit, anything that changes your debt load can kill your loan in underwriting. Do not touch your credit between application and closing.

2. **Changing jobs mid-process.** Lenders verify employment right before closing. A new job — even a better one — can complicate or delay your closing. If you're planning a job change, tell me before you do it.

3. **Skipping the home inspection.** In hot markets, buyers sometimes waive the inspection to make their offer more competitive. This is almost always a mistake. The inspection is your due diligence. Take it.

4. **Buying at the top of your approval amount.** Just because the bank approved you for $400,000 doesn't mean you should spend $400,000. Run the full monthly cost calculation and buy at a payment that doesn't stress your monthly cash flow.

5. **Waiting for the "perfect" market.** The perfect market doesn't exist. Rates are lower than they were, higher than they will be, and imperfect for everyone. The best time to buy is when you are ready — financially and personally. Not when conditions are ideal.

## Florida First-Time Buyer Programs

**Hometown Heroes:** Up to $35,000 in down payment and closing cost assistance. Zero interest, deferred repayment (you pay it back when you sell or refinance). For full-time Florida workers.

**FL Assist:** Up to $10,000, zero interest, deferred.

**FL HFA Preferred Grant:** 3%-5% of the loan amount as a second mortgage, forgiven 20% per year over 5 years.

These programs have income limits and require a HUD-approved homebuyer education course (usually 6-8 hours, often free online). I walk my clients through eligibility every time.
