# Refinancing — When It Makes Sense and When It Doesn't

Refinancing is not always the right move. But when it is right, it can be worth tens of thousands of dollars. Here's how I think about it and how I advise my clients.

---

## The Core Question: Does the Math Work?

Every refinance decision comes down to one calculation: how long does it take to recoup the closing costs through monthly savings?

**The Breakeven Formula:**
Closing Costs ÷ Monthly Savings = Breakeven in Months

Example:
- Current rate: 6.75% on $330,000 balance
- New rate: 6.10%
- New closing costs: $5,500
- Monthly payment reduction: $135/month
- Breakeven: $5,500 ÷ $135 = 40.7 months (just under 3.5 years)

If you plan to stay in the home at least 3.5 more years, this refinance makes sense. If you might sell in 2 years, it doesn't.

---

## When Refinancing Makes Sense

**Rate-and-Term Refinance (Most Common):**
- Your new rate is at least 0.75%-1.0% below your current rate
- You plan to stay in the home past the breakeven point
- Your credit score has improved since the original loan (unlocking better pricing)
- You're removing someone from the loan (divorce, co-borrower situation)

**Cash-Out Refinance:**
- You have significant equity (20%+ after the cash-out)
- The interest rate is lower than alternatives (HELOC, personal loan, credit cards)
- The cash is going toward high-value use (home improvement, investment property, high-interest debt payoff)
- Not for vacations, cars, or lifestyle spending — the math doesn't work long-term

**FHA-to-Conventional Refinance:**
- Your credit score improved to 640+
- You have 20%+ equity — eliminates FHA's lifetime MIP
- Rate improvement is secondary benefit; eliminating MIP is the primary driver
- Potential savings: $100-$200/month in MIP elimination alone

---

## When Refinancing Doesn't Make Sense

- You're past your breakeven point but selling in less than 2 years
- The rate difference is less than 0.50% (closing costs often exceed savings)
- Your credit score has dropped since the original loan
- Your home value has declined and you have limited equity
- You're in the early years of a 30-year loan and have been making extra payments — resetting to a new 30-year clock extends debt payoff significantly

## The 2026 Rate Environment and Refinancing

With rates at 6.35% and forecasts showing possible improvement to the mid-5% range by late 2026 or 2027, here's the refinancing playbook I recommend:

**If you bought in 2021-2023 at 2.5%-4%:** Don't refinance unless you're removing PMI, changing loan terms for a good reason, or taking cash out for a high-value purpose. Your rate is better than the current market. Stay put.

**If you bought in 2023-2024 at 6.5%-7.5%:** Set a rate trigger. The math on refinancing becomes compelling at a 0.75-1.0% reduction. I call this "the refi trigger." I monitor my clients' loans and reach out when we hit their trigger.

**If you bought in 2024-2025 at 6.0%-7.0%:** Same approach — rate trigger. Watch the Fed. Watch inflation data. Be ready to move quickly when the opportunity hits.

## The "Date the Rate, Marry the House" Refinancing Plan

This is the strategy I use with clients buying in today's market:

1. Buy the home at today's rate (6.35%)
2. Set a refi trigger at 5.5% (approximately $150-$200/month savings on a $350K loan)
3. I monitor the market and notify the client when the trigger is reached
4. Refinance — typically breaking even in 18-24 months
5. If rates drop further, refinance again (as long as each refi clears the breakeven test)

This is a disciplined, systematic approach. Not "wait for the perfect rate" — that's paralysis. Not "refinance every time rates twitch" — that's expensive. Set the trigger, execute when it's hit.

## No-Closing-Cost Refinancing

There is no such thing as a truly free refinance. "No closing cost" means the costs are either:
1. Rolled into the loan balance (you're paying them, just financed over 30 years)
2. Offset by a lender credit (you accept a slightly higher rate, lender pays the costs)

Both approaches can be right depending on your situation. If you plan to sell or refinance again within 2-3 years, the no-closing-cost option can make sense — you don't pay costs upfront and won't be there long enough to be hurt by the higher rate or larger balance.
