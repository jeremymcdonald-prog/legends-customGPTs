## Why Rates Are Here

Mortgage rates are driven primarily by the 10-year Treasury yield, not by the Federal Reserve's overnight rate. This is one of the most misunderstood things in mortgage.

Here's the simple version: when investors are nervous about the economy or inflation, they pile into Treasury bonds (safe assets). High demand for bonds drives bond prices up and yields down. When that happens, mortgage rates tend to follow. When the economy looks strong or inflation ticks up, bond demand softens, yields rise, and mortgage rates rise with them.

In 2026, here's what's holding rates where they are:
- **The Fed is on hold.** After a series of cuts in late 2024-2025, the Fed paused. The overnight rate sits at approximately 4.25%-4.50%. The market currently does not expect cuts until September at the earliest.
- **Inflation is sticky.** CPI has cooled from its 2022-2023 highs but hasn't hit the Fed's 2% target cleanly. Tariff uncertainty has added upside risk to inflation, which keeps bond investors cautious.
- **Strong labor market.** When jobs are plentiful and wages are rising, the Fed has less urgency to cut rates to stimulate the economy.

## What I Think Happens Next

I am not a economist and I am not going to pretend I have a crystal ball. Here is the range of scenarios I walk my clients through:

**Base case (most likely):** 1-2 Fed rate cuts in H2 2026, probably starting in September. The 30-year fixed drifts toward 5.75%-6.10% by December 2026. If you buy now at 6.35% and refinance at 5.75% in 12-18 months, you save roughly $120-$150 per month on a $350,000 loan.

**Better case:** Inflation softens faster than expected, Treasury yields drop, mortgage rates hit 5.5%-5.75% by Q4. More buyers enter the market, prices rise. People who bought at 6.35% refinance into the 5% range.

**Worse case:** Tariff inflation, labor market surprises, or a bond market selloff pushes 10-year Treasury yields higher. Mortgage rates drift back toward 6.75%-7.0%. The wait-for-rates strategy backfires — and home prices have risen while you waited.

## What I Tell My Clients

**"Date the rate, marry the house."** This is not just a clever phrase — it is a sound strategy. When you find the right home in the right location for the right price, locking in at 6.35% and refinancing when rates drop is a legitimate financial plan. The home is the long-term asset. The rate is temporary and refinanceable.

**The math on waiting:** On a $350,000 home in Jacksonville, prices have been stable to modestly rising — about 2-3% annual appreciation. If you wait 12 months for rates to potentially drop to 5.75%, the same home now costs $357,000-$360,500. The savings from the lower rate ($120-$150/month) are partially or fully offset by the higher purchase price. Waiting is not always the safe choice.

**The refinance plan:** When I close a loan in today's environment, I set a "refi trigger" with my clients — a target rate (usually 0.75-1.0% below their current rate) where refinancing makes mathematical sense. I monitor their loan and reach out when we hit that trigger. This is part of what I do for every client.

**Rate locks:** Right now I recommend 30-45 day rate locks for buyers in active contract. Rates can move meaningfully in 60-90 days and longer locks cost extra. Get pre-approved, find the home, then lock.

## ARM vs. Fixed — The 2026 Analysis

I'm generally not a fan of ARMs in this environment, and here's why: the rate differential between a 5/1 ARM (6.30%+) and a 30-year fixed (6.35%) is almost nothing right now. You are taking rate risk for essentially no upfront savings.

The one exception: if you know with high confidence you will sell or refinance within 5 years, a 5/1 ARM can make sense. Military buyers who know they'll PCS in 3-4 years, for example. But for a 30-year homeowner, the ARM risk is not worth it at current pricing.
