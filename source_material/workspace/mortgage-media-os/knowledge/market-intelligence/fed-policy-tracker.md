# Fed Policy Tracker: What the Fed Is Doing and Why It Matters to Your Rate

## May 2026 Status

The Federal Reserve has held the federal funds rate at 4.25%-4.50% since December 2024. That's six consecutive meetings with no change. The Fed's language at the May 2025 meeting was "patient" and "data dependent," which is Fed-speak for "we're not moving until we're sure."

The 30-year fixed mortgage rate as of May 2026 is running 6.35%-6.50% for well-qualified borrowers. That number feels high compared to the 3% pandemic era, but it's important context: the historical average for 30-year fixed rates since 1971 is about 7.75%. We're below average, not above it.

## The Fed-to-Mortgage Rate Connection

Here's something that confuses a lot of people. The Fed doesn't set mortgage rates. The Fed sets the federal funds rate, which is what banks charge each other for overnight lending. Mortgage rates are primarily driven by the 10-year Treasury yield.

The relationship works like this:
- Fed policy influences investor expectations
- Those expectations affect bond markets
- The 10-year Treasury yield reflects those expectations
- Mortgage rates track the 10-year Treasury, typically 1.5-2.0 percentage points above it
- As of May 2026, the 10-year Treasury is around 4.40-4.50%, which explains mortgage rates at 6.35-6.50%

What moves the 10-year Treasury? Inflation data (CPI, PCE), employment reports (jobs added, unemployment rate), GDP growth, Federal Reserve statements, and global economic conditions.

## Why the Fed Has Been Holding

The Fed's dual mandate is price stability (roughly 2% inflation) and maximum employment. In 2026, we're sitting with:

- **Inflation:** PCE running around 2.6%-2.8%, above the 2% target but trending down
- **Employment:** Unemployment at 4.1%-4.3%, which is essentially full employment
- **GDP:** Moderate growth, not recessionary

The Fed is in a difficult spot. Inflation isn't back to target yet, so cutting rates risks re-igniting it. But holding rates too long risks slowing the economy unnecessarily. Their response has been to hold and watch.

## The Rate Cut Forecast

Based on Fed statements and market pricing as of May 2026, here's the consensus view:

**Most likely scenario (45% probability):** 1-2 rate cuts in late 2026 (Q3-Q4), totaling 0.25%-0.50% reduction. Mortgage rates drift down to 5.80%-6.10% by year end.

**Better scenario (25% probability):** Inflation drops faster than expected, Fed cuts 3 times in 2026. Mortgage rates reach 5.50%-5.80% by end of year.

**Worse scenario (30% probability):** Inflation proves sticky or a new economic shock occurs. Fed holds all year. Mortgage rates stay 6.25%-6.75%.

I'm not making a prediction here and nobody should. But understanding the range of outcomes helps you make a smarter decision about when to buy and whether to lock.

## What This Means for Buyers Right Now

I get this question every week: "Should I wait for rates to come down?"

Here's my honest take. Nobody timed the 2020-2021 rate market perfectly. Nobody is going to time this one perfectly either. What I tell clients is this:

**If you're buying to live in the home:** Buy when you're ready. The home you want might not exist in 6 months. Prices in Northeast Florida have stayed firm even as rates rose. If rates drop, we refinance. If they don't, you locked in at today's price before the next move up.

**If you're buying as an investment:** Run the DSCR numbers at current rates. If the property cash-flows at today's rates, it's a good deal regardless of what rates do next. If it only works at theoretical future rates, that's speculation, not investing.

**If you're refinancing:** The math is simple. Monthly savings divided by closing costs equals breakeven in months. If you'll stay in the home longer than the breakeven point and rates drop 0.75%+, the refinance conversation makes sense. I'll run those numbers for you anytime.

Buying now, refinancing later, has historically been a better strategy than timing the market.

## Key Economic Indicators to Watch

These are the reports that move mortgage rates most significantly:

1. **Monthly Jobs Report (first Friday of each month):** Strong jobs = rates rise. Weak jobs = rates fall.
2. **CPI Report (monthly):** High inflation = rates rise. Cooling inflation = rates fall.
3. **PCE Index (monthly):** The Fed's preferred inflation measure. Same dynamic as CPI.
4. **GDP Growth (quarterly):** Strong growth can push rates up. Recession fears pull them down.
5. **FOMC Statements:** Hawkish language (focused on fighting inflation) = rates rise. Dovish language = rates fall.
