## The Core Argument (30-Second Version)

When you go to your bank for a mortgage, you're asking a company that sells one product what the best product is. Of course they'll say theirs is best — they don't have anything else to sell you.

When you come to me, I'm a licensed broker with access to 40+ wholesale lenders competing for your loan. I find the best rate, the best terms, and the best fit for your specific situation. And I back it with a $1,000 guarantee. If I can't beat your bank's offer, I'll write you a check.

That's the difference.

## The Data

**Average savings using a broker vs. a bank:** $3,000-$8,000 over the life of the loan (industry average)

**Concrete rate example:** If your bank quotes 6.35% and I find 6.09% from a wholesale lender, that's:
- $47/month less in payments
- $16,920 less over 30 years
- On a $350,000 loan — just because you came to me instead of staying at your bank

**Why wholesale rates are lower than retail rates:**
Wholesale lenders don't have branch networks, national ad campaigns, or retail overhead. They exist to lend to brokers at wholesale pricing. A broker with volume relationships gets pricing that no retail consumer ever sees walking into a bank branch.

**Broker market share:** Mortgage brokers originated approximately 25-30% of all mortgages in 2026. That share is growing because consumers who use brokers keep coming back.

You go to Expedia, or Google Flights, or a travel agent who searches across all airlines simultaneously and finds you the best deal.

## Objection: "My bank will give me a loyalty discount"

**Response:** I hear this a lot. And sometimes it's true — banks occasionally offer existing customers a small rate incentive. But here's what I'd ask you to do: get that loyalty offer in writing. Then let me see it. In 20 years, the "loyalty discount" has beaten what I can find from wholesale lenders exactly zero times.

Banks want you to believe the loyalty discount is significant because it stops you from shopping. Don't let a small courtesy override thousands of dollars in savings.

---

## Objection: "A broker takes a commission and that makes them more expensive"

**Response:** This is a really smart question and I'm glad you asked it, because the answer surprises most people.

Broker compensation is disclosed on the Loan Estimate — it's legally required. It is built into the interest rate or paid directly by the lender. I don't add cost on top of what the lender is charging — I'm extracting the best available price from the lender's own pricing grid.

When you go to a bank, their loan officer also earns a commission — it's just not disclosed separately because it's buried in the bank's pricing model. So you're paying for a commission either way. The difference is that I'm shopping 40+ lenders to find the one with the best pricing, and then my compensation comes out of that pricing. The bank's loan officer is only selling you the one product their bank offers.

Net result: broker typically costs you less, not more.

---

## Objection: "The bank will be faster because everything is in-house"

**Response:** Modern mortgage lending has almost entirely moved to electronic submission and automated underwriting. The closing timeline difference between a broker and a bank in 2026 is usually 2-3 days, not weeks. Both are targeting 21-30 day closings.

Where brokers actually have an advantage: when something unusual happens in your file. A bank with one underwriting team and one set of guidelines either approves you or doesn't. I have 40+ lenders with different guidelines. If one lender declines, I pivot to the next. The flexibility is the advantage.

## Objection: "I trust my bank — I've been with them for 20 years"

**Response:** Your relationship with your bank for checking and savings is great. That doesn't mean they're going to give you the best mortgage rate. They're a different department with a different profit motive.

Would you stay with one car insurance company for 20 years without shopping the renewal? Of course not — you know that shopping saves money. Mortgage is the same. Your loyalty is worth exactly what they offer you, not a dollar more.

## Broker-Specific Advantages to Emphasize

**Access:** 40+ wholesale lenders, including specialty programs (non-QM, bank statement, DSCR, VA, USDA) that banks don't offer

**Flexibility:** When you don't fit a bank's cookie-cutter guidelines (self-employed, investor, credit complexity), brokers pivot to lenders who specialize in your situation

**Transparency:** Broker compensation is disclosed by law. You see exactly what I earn.

**Service:** My income depends on your referrals. Your bank loan officer's income does not. I have a structural incentive to treat you extraordinarily well because my business runs on word of mouth.

**Expertise:** Brokers who survive in this market know it deeply. I'm not a generalist — I'm a mortgage specialist with 20 years of experience and a NMLS license that requires continuing education to maintain.

## The Fiduciary Frame

While brokers are not technically fiduciaries (a legal designation), we operate in a fiduciary-like manner. I am legally prohibited from recommending a loan that is not in your best interest. I am required to disclose my compensation. And my business model depends on your satisfaction — if you're unhappy, you don't refer me anyone, and I'm out of business.

Your bank loan officer is an employee of the bank. Their loyalty is to the bank's profitability. That's not a character flaw — it's just an organizational reality.
